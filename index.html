Lifespan interviewing & counselling

Workshop 8 extended learning materials — Dr Olivia Wright PhD AdvAPD

Expand each life stage for extended clinical notes, evidence-based context, and practical counselling strategies to supplement the workshop slides.

Iron, zinc & calcium — the priority nutrients
These three nutrients are critical during the pre-school years. Iron deficiency anaemia remains prevalent and impairs cognitive development — always assess red meat, legumes, fortified cereals, and vitamin C intake (which enhances non-haem iron absorption). Calcium needs are 700 mg/day (age 1–3) and 1000 mg/day (age 4–8). Watch for excessive milk intake displacing iron-rich foods — a common clinical presentation.
Food neophobia is developmentally normal
Food neophobia (fear of new foods) peaks between ages 2–6. Research demonstrates that 8–15 exposures may be required before acceptance — parents often give up after 3–4 attempts. Validate parental frustration, then reframe: repeated, low-pressure exposure in a calm setting is evidence-based. Tasting without any obligation to swallow is a valid and valuable step.
Satter's Division of Responsibility
A cornerstone of paediatric nutrition counselling. Parents are responsible for WHAT food is offered, WHEN, and WHERE. Children are responsible for WHETHER they eat and HOW MUCH. This model reduces mealtime coercion, which is associated with poorer long-term food relationships, fussiness, and both under- and over-eating in later childhood.
Sensory vs nutrient language
Pre-schoolers classify food by colour, shape, and texture — not nutrients. Use sensory language ("this is crunchy like crackers — want to try?") rather than health framing ("this has lots of vitamins"). Engage their exploratory nature: touching, smelling, and helping prepare food all count as meaningful steps toward eventual acceptance.
Screen time, media & eating behaviour
Distracted eating during screen use impairs satiety cue recognition — children eat beyond fullness without noticing. Australian guidelines recommend no screens during meals and a maximum of 1 hour/day of screen time in this age group. Media exposure to food advertising begins shaping food preferences from as early as age 2.
The school food environment
Children make increasingly independent food choices at school. Canteen access, packed lunch contents, and peer eating norms all shape intake significantly. Broaden your assessment beyond the home environment — ask specifically about school lunches, tuck shop habits, and what friends eat. Consider the school food policy as a systems-level lever in population interventions.
Family meals as a protective factor
Strong epidemiological evidence links regular family meals (even 3x/week) with healthier dietary quality, lower obesity risk, and reduced risk of disordered eating. Family meals provide structure, behavioural modelling, and social connection — all of which influence eating behaviour. This is one of the most impactful single recommendations for this age group.
Weight-sensitive language is essential
This age group is highly susceptible to weight stigma and the early formation of disordered eating patterns. Always focus on energy for sport, mood, concentration, skin health, and immune function — never on weight or body size. The "healthy weight" discourse in school settings requires careful, evidence-informed navigation to avoid unintended harm.
Food advertising & media literacy
Children aged 6–12 are exposed to approximately 10–15 food advertisements daily, predominantly for discretionary foods. Incorporating media literacy into nutrition education builds critical thinking skills about food marketing. This empowers children to evaluate food claims independently — a skill with lifelong protective value.
Action-based engagement works best
Abstract nutritional concepts do not resonate well at this developmental stage. Hands-on cooking, school food gardens, food photography diaries, and interactive apps are significantly more effective than verbal or written nutrition education. When children grow, prepare, or photograph food they are more likely to try and enjoy it.
Peak nutrient demands — frame them strategically
Adolescence represents a lifetime peak for iron (particularly females post-menarche), calcium (bone mineralisation phase), and zinc. Despite these high requirements, dietary quality often declines during this period due to increased autonomy and exposure to fast food and processed snacks. Frame nutrients through what adolescents care about: iron → energy and sport performance; calcium → bone strength and injury prevention; zinc → skin health.
Body image — the clinical imperative
Adolescence is the highest-risk developmental period for the onset of eating disorders. Any weight- or body-related conversation must be handled with extreme care. The Health at Every Size® (HAES) framework and weight-neutral counselling language are strongly recommended. Never comment on weight or body shape unprompted. Always assess for disordered eating risk before initiating any dietary intervention.
Motivational Interviewing is essential here
Adolescents are developmentally primed to resist adult direction — this is healthy identity formation, not obstruction. MI is particularly powerful because it respects autonomy. Ask permission before giving information. Use reflective listening. Help them identify the discrepancy between current behaviour and their own values (sport performance, friendship, feeling good) — never yours or their parents' values.
Social media & diet culture
TikTok, Instagram, and YouTube expose adolescents to intermittent fasting, "clean eating," detox diets, and extreme physiques daily. Don't dismiss this — acknowledge it directly and with genuine curiosity. Build critical evaluation skills alongside nutrition knowledge. Connecting evidence-based information to their existing media landscape is far more effective than ignoring it.
Tailor approach to sub-stage
Early adolescence (12–14): family-focused approaches, clear and concrete messages, educational materials. Mid-adolescence (15–17): leverage emerging abstract thinking for pros/cons analysis, problem-solving, and decision-making frameworks. Late adolescence (18–19): increasing complexity, life transitions, building independent food skills — cooking, budgeting, label reading, navigating food environments independently.
Sarcopenia & protein — increased needs, different targets
Muscle loss (sarcopenia) accelerates after age 65. Protein requirements increase to 1.0–1.2 g/kg/day — higher than the 0.8 g/kg recommended for younger adults. Critically, protein distribution across meals matters more than total intake: spreading protein across 3–4 meals (each providing 25–30 g) is more anabolic than a single large serve. Leucine-rich foods (meat, fish, eggs, dairy, legumes) are particularly important for muscle protein synthesis.
Polypharmacy-nutrition interactions
Many common medications directly affect nutritional status. Key interactions: metformin depletes vitamin B12; diuretics deplete potassium and magnesium; warfarin interacts with vitamin K foods; proton pump inhibitors impair iron and B12 absorption; corticosteroids increase calcium excretion; many antibiotics affect gut microbiome and vitamin K production. Always take a comprehensive medication history and consider nutrient-drug interactions as part of nutritional assessment.
Sensory changes affecting intake
Taste (particularly salty and sweet) and smell decline with age, making food less appealing and reducing appetite. Zinc deficiency — common in older adults — worsens taste perception. Address this by enhancing flavour through herbs, spices, umami-rich foods (cheese, tomato, mushrooms), and meal variety. Always assess dentition — poor oral health significantly restricts food choices and nutritional adequacy and warrants dental referral.
Social isolation as a nutrition risk factor
The health impact of social isolation is comparable to smoking 15 cigarettes per day. Eating alone is strongly associated with reduced appetite, reduced meal quality, and lower overall dietary diversity. Social prescription — connecting clients with community dining programmes, meals-on-wheels, lunch clubs, or other social groups — can be as clinically impactful as any dietary change, and should be considered a core intervention component.
Person-centred practice — food is identity
Older adults bring decades of food culture, identity, and memory to the consultation. Always begin with their food story — what meals meant to them, who they cooked for, what they used to enjoy. Build recommendations onto existing preferences, routines, and capabilities. Practical barriers (transport, cooking capacity, income, cognitive decline, physical limitations) often require addressing before dietary quality can meaningfully improve. Don't skip to dietary advice before understanding the full context.

Expand each topic for deeper coverage of CBT principles and their application in nutrition and lifestyle counselling, supplementing the cognitive aspects of Workshop 8.

A — Activating event
The situation or trigger. In nutrition contexts: eating an unplanned food, missing planned exercise, a social eating event, seeing a number on the scale, receiving a comment about eating or body. Critically — the event itself is not what determines the emotional or behavioural response. Two people can experience the same activating event and respond completely differently depending on their beliefs.
B — Beliefs / Automatic thoughts
The interpretation of the event — rapid, often unconscious, and experienced as fact. "I've ruined everything." "I have no willpower." "What's the point." "Everyone noticed." These thoughts feel like objective reality to the person experiencing them, but they are interpretations shaped by core beliefs, past experiences, and cognitive habits — not facts about the situation.
C — Consequences
The emotional reactions (shame, guilt, frustration, hopelessness) and behavioural responses (restriction, bingeing, avoidance, giving up) that follow from B — not directly from A. This is the key therapeutic insight: it is the belief about what happened that drives behaviour, not the event itself. Changing B changes C — without changing the external circumstances at all.
Clinical application for dietitians
When a client describes a dietary "slip" or setback, explore B before responding to C. Ask: "What did you tell yourself when that happened?" or "What went through your mind in that moment?" This opens the cognitive work and reveals the beliefs that need addressing — rather than reinforcing the narrative that the food itself (A) was the problem. Many clients have never had anyone ask them this question.
All-or-nothing (black & white) thinking
The most common cognitive distortion in dietary behaviour change. "I ate chips so my diet is ruined." Any deviation from a plan equals total failure. This fuels the restrict → lapse → binge → shame → restrict cycle. Watch for absolutes: "completely," "total," "ruined," "blown it," "failed." One imperfect meal in a week of good eating is objectively a 93% success — but all-or-nothing thinking makes it feel like zero.
Catastrophising
"If I can't stick to this I'll never be healthy." Magnifying the significance and permanence of a single event. The past is treated as a reliable predictor of all future outcomes. Listen for words like "always," "never," "everyone," "completely," "forever" — these flag catastrophising language that needs gentle examination.
Should/must statements
Rigid food rules create shame and failure when inevitably broken. "I should never eat after 8pm." "I must be perfect on weekdays." "I shouldn't eat carbs." These rules often originate in diet culture rather than evidence. When a client uses "should," ask: "Where does that rule come from? What happens when you break it? Is it serving you well?" Rigid rules are incompatible with flexible, sustainable eating.
Emotional reasoning
"I feel disgusting after eating that, so it must have been terrible for me." Treating feelings as evidence of facts. Common after consuming "forbidden" foods — the guilt feels proportional to a serious health transgression even when the intake was moderate. The feeling is real; what it proves about the food or the person is not.
Discounting the positive
A client who follows their meal plan on 6 out of 7 days but focuses exclusively on the one difficult day. Positive progress is dismissed ("that doesn't count, anyone could do that") while negatives are amplified. Cognitive restructuring begins by making the positives as visible, concrete, and valid as the negatives — not by dismissing the difficulty, but by restoring proportion.
Record — capture the thought precisely
Write the automatic thought in the client's own words, in first person, exactly as it occurred. "I'm a failure. I ate birthday cake and ruined my diabetes management." Specificity is critical — vague records are harder to examine and challenge. The thought diary approach (recording situation, feelings 0–100, thought, and the "hottest" most distressing thought) provides structure for this process.
Rationalise — examine the evidence
Socratic questions to examine the thought: What is the evidence that supports it? What evidence challenges it? Is it logical, realistic, and fair? Is it a compassionate way of thinking about yourself? What would you say to a close friend in exactly this situation? What would a reasonable, caring observer say? What does the actual data show — the food diary, the readings, the adherence record?
Replace — develop a balanced alternative
The goal is accuracy — not toxic positivity. The replacement thought must be honest and believable to the client, not a cheerful slogan they don't believe. Example: Original: "I'm a complete failure — I ate birthday cake and ruined everything." Balanced alternative: "I had one slice of cake at my son's birthday. My readings this week are still in target range. One food choice does not determine my health outcomes. I can return to my usual eating pattern at the next meal."
The clinical reminder
The dietitian guides the discovery through Socratic questioning — they do NOT correct the thought or provide the replacement for the client. Insight generated by the client themselves is far more durable, motivating, and generalisable than insight provided by a clinician. Your job is to ask good questions, not to supply good answers. Curiosity over correction; guided discovery over persuasion.
S — Stop
Pause when you notice distressing thoughts or urges arising. Create a deliberate gap between stimulus and response. Even a 5-second pause interrupts the automatic behavioural chain — the space between impulse and action is where change becomes possible. Simply noticing "I'm having a strong thought right now" creates cognitive distance.
T — Take a breath
One slow, deliberate deep breath activates the parasympathetic nervous system, reducing physiological arousal and literally restoring prefrontal cortex (thinking brain) function that is suppressed during stress. This is not a relaxation technique — it is a neurological reset. The breath is a tool, not a metaphor.
O — Observe
Notice what the mind is saying and what the body is doing — with curiosity, not judgement. "There goes my mind telling me I've failed again." Observation creates distance between the self and the thought. Thoughts become events to observe, not commands to obey. This is the mindfulness component of CBT — witnessing experience rather than being consumed by it.
P — Pull back & Perspective
See the bigger picture. Is this thought a fact or an interpretation? What would I say to a friend in this moment? How significant will this feel in a week, a month, a year? Consider other possible explanations. Zoom out from the immediate distress to the wider context of who you are, what you have achieved, and what actually matters to you.
P — Practice what works & Proceed
Choose the response most aligned with your values and long-term goals — not the most automatic, habitual, or immediately gratifying one. With regular practice (including during calm moments, not just crisis), STOPP becomes habitual and begins interrupting distress cycles before they escalate. The STOPP app and getselfhelp.co.uk provide client-facing resources and worksheets.
Why self-efficacy predicts behaviour change
Self-efficacy — the belief in one's ability to perform a specific behaviour in a specific situation — is one of the strongest predictors of dietary behaviour change. Importantly, it is situation-specific: a client may have high efficacy for cooking at home but very low efficacy for making healthy choices at a work morning tea. Always assess efficacy for the specific behaviour, not globally.
Source 1: Mastery experiences
Past successes are the most powerful source of self-efficacy. Help clients identify times they have achieved a dietary or health-related goal, even partially. "Tell me about a time when you managed to eat well even when it was difficult." Graded task assignment — setting goals that are achievable before progressing to harder ones — deliberately builds mastery experiences and therefore efficacy.
Sources 2–4: Vicarious, verbal & physiological
Vicarious experiences ("people like me can do this") make peer support and group interventions powerful. Verbal persuasion — affirmation from a trusted clinician — helps, but should be specific and credible, not generic cheerleading. Physiological states matter: anxiety, fatigue, and hunger reduce perceived efficacy. Addressing practical barriers (sleep, stress, meal prep) builds the conditions in which efficacy can grow.
Clinical application — the confidence ruler
Before finalising a goal, assess efficacy: "On a scale of 0–10, how confident are you that you could achieve this goal over the next week?" A score below 7 signals the goal needs revision — not the client. Ask: "What would need to be different for this to feel more achievable?" Revise the goal until the client reaches 7+. This single step dramatically improves follow-through and prevents demoralising failure experiences.

Lifespan — Pre-school Case study 1: The picky pre-schooler
You are a dietitian seeing 4-year-old Aiden with his mother, Priya. Priya reports that Aiden has been on a "pasta-only kick" for 6 weeks — refusing all other foods and having a meltdown whenever a new food appears on his plate. She is anxious about nutritional deficiencies. She has started making separate meals for him and bribing him with dessert to eat "a few bites" of other foods. Dad thinks they should "just let him eat pasta until he gets sick of it."
✓ Model answer key — Case study 1

Developmental context

Food jags (eating only one food repetitively) and food neophobia are developmentally normal in children aged 2–6 years. Aiden is asserting autonomy through food — a hallmark of this stage. The reduced rate of growth compared to infancy means energy needs are lower and appetite is naturally more variable. This is not defiance; it is development. Food neophobia typically peaks at 2–6 years and requires 8–15 low-pressure exposures before a new food is accepted.

Concerning elements to assess

While food jags are normal, assess iron, zinc, and calcium status (dietary and if warranted, biochemical). Watch for signs of micronutrient deficiency: pallor, fatigue, frequent illness (iron/zinc), or poor growth. Enriched pasta does provide some iron and B vitamins — ask about brand and whether Priya is adding any protein or sauce. Also assess whether Aiden is drinking excessive milk, which can displace iron-rich foods.

Strategies — Satter's Division of Responsibility

Introduce Satter's Division of Responsibility: Priya decides WHAT is offered, WHEN, and WHERE; Aiden decides whether and how much he eats. Recommend serving pasta alongside (not instead of) other family foods without pressure. Stop the separate meal preparation — this reinforces the jag. Discourage dessert as a reward (coercive feeding) as it elevates the value of the "reward" food and can worsen the problem long-term.

Navigating parental differences

Validate both parents' instincts — Priya's concern is appropriate and caring; Dad's "let it go" approach has some merit (low pressure is evidence-based). Reframe the recommendation as a middle path: consistent structure and family meals, no pressure to eat the new food, but continued exposure on the plate without comment. Involving Aiden in food selection and simple meal preparation (stirring, picking the sauce) can increase engagement without pressure.

Lifespan — Adolescence Case study 2: The disengaged adolescent
Mia is a 15-year-old referred by her GP for fatigue and low iron. She attends with her mother but is clearly reluctant — she uses her phone throughout. When asked about eating she says: "I skip breakfast, have chips and a Red Bull at lunch, then whatever Mum makes for dinner but I usually go to my room." She plays netball 3x/week. Her mother interjects frequently. Mia rolls her eyes each time.
✓ Model answer key — Case study 2

Developmental context

Mia is in mid-adolescence — a stage defined by increasing autonomy, peer influence, identity development, and resistance to parental/adult authority. Her disengagement is not unusual; it is developmentally expected. The phone use and eye-rolling reflect that she feels this appointment was not her choice. Resistance is information, not obstruction. Her iron deficiency is likely contributing to the fatigue, which may be your best motivational hook — she plays netball and presumably cares about her performance.

Managing the parent dynamic

Early in the session, acknowledge the mother's care and concern, then redirect: "It's wonderful you're here supporting Mia. To help me understand things from Mia's perspective, would you be okay if I spoke with her directly for a little while?" Consider asking the mother to briefly step out for part of the session. Avoid appearing to "side" with Mia against her mother — frame the one-to-one time as standard practice, not a judgement on the mother.

MI techniques for this session

Use open questions that invite Mia's perspective without judgement: "What's netball like for you at the moment?" "When do you feel at your best energy — what's your day usually like then?" Reflective listening: "So you're tired a lot — that sounds pretty frustrating when you want to train." Ask permission before giving information: "Would it be okay if I shared something about what I often see in players who feel the way you do?" Explore the discrepancy gently: "You mentioned you want to play better — what do you think gets in the way of that?"

Realistic first goal

A single, small, self-chosen change with high confidence (7+/10) is far more valuable than an ambitious plan. A realistic starting point might be: adding one iron-containing food to her lunch (e.g., switching one day to a chicken wrap), or having a small breakfast before netball training only. Frame it around energy and performance, not nutrition or health. Let Mia choose and name the goal — ownership drives follow-through.

Cognitions & CBT Case study 3: The cognitive saboteur
David, 48, has type 2 diabetes. He arrives looking defeated: "I've completely blown it this week. There was a morning tea at work and I had two pieces of cake. I just can't do this. I've been trying for 3 months and I always end up back at square one. I'm clearly a person who just can't control themselves around food." His food diary shows he followed his meal plan on 6 out of 7 days this week.
✓ Model answer key — Case study 3

Cognitive distortions present

All-or-nothing thinking: "I've completely blown it" and "I always end up back at square one" — his food diary objectively shows 6/7 days of adherence (86%), which is excellent. One difficult day is being framed as total failure. Overgeneralising: "I always end up back at square one" — three months of effort is being dismissed based on one event. Labelling: "I'm clearly a person who just can't control themselves" — David has defined his whole identity based on a single behaviour. Discounting the positive: Six days of successful adherence are being completely ignored.

Socratic questions to explore the thoughts

"David, I'm looking at your diary here — can you help me understand what 'square one' looks like to you?" / "You mentioned you've been trying for 3 months. What has actually changed in that time?" / "If a friend came to you and said they'd stuck to their plan 6 out of 7 days, what would you tell them?" / "What does your food diary from this week actually show — can we look at it together?" These questions invite David to examine the evidence himself rather than being corrected by you.

RRR applied to David's hot thought

Record: "I'm clearly a person who just can't control themselves around food." Rationalise: Evidence against — food diary shows 6/7 days of adherence this week; 3 months of sustained effort; this is the first major lapse he has reported; he attended the appointment today despite feeling defeated. Evidence for — he ate 2 pieces of cake at morning tea. Is one lapse over 3 months evidence that he "can't control himself"? Is it logical? Is it fair? Replace: "I had a difficult moment at a work event. My diary shows I've managed well on 86% of days this week and for 3 months. One challenging situation doesn't cancel that out. I can focus on what I do well and return to my plan at the next meal."

Lifespan — Older adults Case study 4: Older adult complexity
Evelyn is 78, referred by her GP for unintentional weight loss of 5 kg over 3 months. She lives alone following the death of her husband 8 months ago. She takes 9 medications. She reports: "I used to cook for two — now I can't be bothered just for myself. Things don't taste the same. The television is my company at mealtimes." Assessment findings include reduced grip strength, mild dehydration, and poor dentition.
✓ Model answer key — Case study 4

Categorisation of factors

Physical: Reduced grip strength (sarcopenia risk), poor dentition (limits food choices and chewing capacity), unintentional weight loss (5 kg over 3 months is clinically significant), possible frailty. Social: Social isolation, eating alone, bereavement — eating for one is a known risk factor for reduced dietary intake and quality. Psychological: Grief (husband died 8 months ago), possible depression (low motivation, "can't be bothered"), loss of purpose and identity as a cook/caregiver. Physiological: Taste/smell changes (common with age), polypharmacy (9 medications — consider B12, potassium, appetite interactions), reduced appetite, possible malabsorption, mild dehydration. Environmental: Cooking for one reduces motivation; meal planning and preparation designed for two is no longer relevant.

Prioritisation and strategies

First priority: dental referral (without adequate dentition, dietary change is severely limited) and GP discussion about medication review for appetite/nutrition effects. Second priority: address social isolation — social prescription (community dining, lunch clubs, meals with family). Research shows eating with others significantly improves intake. Third priority: practical food strategies — nutrient-dense, small-volume meals requiring minimal preparation; fortified foods; protein distribution across meals to address sarcopenia risk. Assess for depression and refer to GP or psychologist if indicated — treating underlying low mood may be the most impactful nutritional intervention.

Counselling approach adaptations

Begin with Evelyn's food story — what she used to cook, what meals meant to her and her husband. This builds rapport, reveals her capabilities and values, and positions her as the expert in her own life. Use visuals and simple written summaries rather than complex dietary information. Allow more time; don't rush to solutions. Address practical barriers (can she physically shop, prepare food, open packaging?) before dietary advice. Be alert to grief — acknowledge loss explicitly. Practical, compassionate, and person-centred care is the framework; dietary advice is a tool within it, not the starting point.
